Free Will


Research shows that free will might just be an illusion, because our brains make decisions before we even notice them.

When people talk about free will, they usually mean the idea that they, as conscious individuals, can make choices on their own, without being completely controlled by outside forces or past events. It's the feeling that in any situation, they could have chosen differently if they really wanted to. Basically, it's that sense of saying, “I am the one making this decision.”

Even though this belief feels natural, decades of research suggest that our conscious minds might not actually be the ones controlling our actions.

One of the most well-known studies is Benjamin Libet's experiments from the 1980s. He measured brain activity called the “readiness potential” before participants moved their fingers. He found that this activity started about half a second before people said they had decided to move. This shows that the brain begins preparing an action before we are even aware we've made the choice.

In 2008, Soon and colleagues used brain scans to predict which button people would press 7 to 10 seconds before they even realized they had made the choice. It shows the brain starts leaning toward a decision long before we're consciously aware of it. A few years later, in 2013, Soon and colleagues showed the same thing happens with more complex decisions, like choosing whether to add or subtract numbers.

Psychologist Daniel Wegner, in his 2002 book The Illusion of Conscious Will, argued that the feeling we're consciously in control of our actions can be misleading. In many experiments, people thought they were causing actions that were actually triggered or influenced by external factors. Wegner suggests that our consciousness often just takes credit for actions the brain has already started, giving us the sense of “I willed this” even though we weren't the true cause.

Both classical physics and quantum physics challenge the idea of free will. Classical physics tells us that everything in the universe follows strict cause and effect, so if you could rewind time and everything was exactly the same, you would always make the same choices. That means your decisions aren't really “free” in the way we usually think. Quantum physics adds a little randomness at the tiniest levels, but randomness isn't the same as control. Just because something happens by chance doesn't mean you're actively choosing it. So whether the universe is predictable or a bit random, it still doesn't give us the kind of free will we usually imagine.
